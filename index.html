The problem

A retail network flying
completely blind.

It’s a Friday evening. A family walks into a store to buy their favorite brand of premium juice for the weekend. The shelf is completely empty. They leave and buy it at a competitor's store.

The store manager investigates and finds the system says 0 stock. They place an emergency order immediately, but the upstream supplier has an 11-day lead time. The next weekend arrives—foot traffic is up 40% for Saturday shopping, but the product still hasn't arrived. The shelves remain empty. The cycle repeats.

Meanwhile, across 478 stores, $47.4M in revenue is silently disappearing. Not from theft. Not from damage. From a forecasting model that treats every day the same — a 30-day moving average that has no concept of weekends, promotions, lead time variability, or demand uncertainty. When Black Friday hits and stores clear shelves in 4 hours, the model keeps predicting the same volumes it saw last month. For every 10 units sold, it predicted 17. Bias: -1.72.

The safety stock policy was Target = μ. Average demand, nothing more. No buffer for uncertainty. No tier-based service level. No model for what happens when a supplier delivers 3 days late into a peak week. The result: stockouts that were entirely predictable if you had the right model.

The core problem: Legacy systems produce numbers, not intelligence. They tell you what demand was. They don't tell you what it will be, how uncertain that prediction is, where ghost inventory is locking up capital, or what to do about any of it. HyperShelf DemandSense v2 was built to fix all four.

Demand Forecasting

LightGBM — why gradient
boosting wins in retail.

Retail demand is non-linear, non-stationary, and event-driven. A 30-day moving average assumes yesterday predicts tomorrow. LightGBM assumes nothing — it learns from the interaction between day-of-week, promotional depth, lag velocity, and store format simultaneously, capturing patterns that no linear model can represent.

Why LightGBM specifically

Gradient boosting builds an ensemble of trees sequentially — each tree corrects the residual errors of the previous one. For retail, this matters because demand drivers interact in complex, non-additive ways: a Saturday promotion in a Premium store during summer hits differently than the same promotion on a Tuesday in a Medium store in winter. Decision trees capture those interactions natively through splits. Linear models require you to specify them manually, and you'll miss most of them.

LightGBM specifically — rather than XGBoost — was chosen for its leaf-wise tree growth on 4.58 GB of training data. Leaf-wise growth reduces error faster per tree than level-wise, which matters when training on 18.3 million time-series observations where every additional epoch is computationally expensive.

Probabilistic output: Instead of predicting a single demand number (P50), the model uses quantile regression to output P10 (low demand scenario), P50 (median expected), and P90 (high demand scenario). The P90 drives safety stock decisions — order at the upper bound to protect against demand spikes. This alone restored P90 coverage from 32.7% to 89.75%.

22 features — what each does

Segment featurescategory, foot_traffic_tier, region, store_format, price_band — the model learns separate demand baselines per segment. Premium stores in the Southwest have fundamentally different demand curves than Low-tier stores in the Northeast.
Temporal featuresdow, month, weekofyear, is_weekend — retail demand is weekly-seasonal. Saturday demand for beverages is 40% higher than Monday. Without weekday features, the model can't capture this and over-orders on Mondays, under-orders on weekends.
Lag featureslag_1, lag_7, lag_14, lag_28 — give the model memory. lag_7 says "what did this SKU sell exactly one week ago?" This captures the weekly demand cycle. roll_mean_7/28 smooth out noise. roll_std_7 captures demand volatility — high std → wider safety stock band needed.
Promo features (V2.2)discount_pct, demand_lift_factor, promo_depth_x_flag, promo_lift_x_flag — the legacy model had zero promo awareness. A 40%-off event causing 3× demand looked identical to a regular Tuesday. These 4 features reduced promotional shortages from 97.8 units average to near zero.
Stockout proxy (V2.2)lost_units_proxy, stockout_flag — when a shelf is empty, sales are zero even if demand is high. Without this, the model trains on censored observations and under-forecasts during restocking. The proxy estimates true demand during stockout periods.

The fundamental error — how money is lost

In supply chain forecasting, the basic calculation defines exactly how you lose money:

Forecast Error Definition
Error = Actual Demand - Forecast
Positive Error (Under-forecasting): Actual > Forecast. Leads directly to stockouts and lost sales.
Negative Error (Over-forecasting): Forecast > Actual. Leads to excess inventory, locked capital, and waste.

Our analysis revealed that the old moving average model heavily skewed toward positive errors during high-footfall promotional events. This under-forecasting is what drove the massive $85.6M in baseline revenue leakage.

The true demand problem — the evolution of the WAPE metric

The legacy moving average model had a massive error rate of around 32%. We initially built a LightGBM model that brought the standard WAPE down to 24.18%. However, we realized this was a false success. The standard WAPE calculation uses observed sales as the target. But when shelves are empty, sales are zero regardless of actual demand. This censors the training signal. When we corrected the target to True Demand (actual sales + lost units), we found that the 24.18% model actually had an error of 28.19%. We then retrained the pipeline completely on the True Demand target, successfully bringing this much harder metric down to a final 24.06%.

The NULL promotion bug — why only 1,434 rows had promo data

promotions.csv stored all-store promotions with store_id = NULL. A direct join on store_id dropped every all-store promo from the training data — leaving only 1,434 non-zero discount_pct rows out of 24 million. The model trained as if almost nothing was ever on promotion. Fix: detect NULL store_id rows, expand them to every store in the network before joining, then re-merge with store-specific promos. The promo feature went from near-useless to one of the top-5 by feature importance.

Phantom Inventory Detection

Random Forest — hunting
ghosts in the data.

Phantom inventory is product the system believes exists but physically doesn't — miscounted, mislabelled, lost behind shelves. The system places no reorders because inventory shows available. The customer finds empty shelves. 14,041 such SKUs were flagged across 478 stores with High/Medium/Low confidence.

Why Random Forest for this problem

Phantom detection is a binary classification problem with highly non-linear decision boundaries. A product that sold 50 units last week and zero this week is probably not a phantom — maybe it's a slow week. But a product that shows 200 units available, matches 3 phantom detection rules, and has had zero sales for 14 consecutive days is almost certainly a ghost. Random Forest captures these compound patterns through feature interactions across many trees, naturally handling class imbalance and providing confidence scores through vote proportion rather than a hard threshold.

Why F2, not F1 or accuracy: In phantom detection, a False Negative means missing a real ghost — that product stays "in stock," no reorder fires, shelves stay empty, revenue is lost. A False Positive means sending an associate to check an aisle that turns out to be fine — 2 minutes wasted. The cost asymmetry is extreme. F2 weights recall twice as heavily as precision, accepting more false positives to catch more real phantoms.

4 engineering decisions that made it work

Label sourceOriginal labels came from the stockout table, which only captures complete stockouts — shelves fully empty. Ghost inventory is different: the system shows stock, but nothing sells. Labels were rebuilt from inventory-sales mismatch: stock present in system + zero observed sales velocity. This fundamentally changed what the model was learning.
All 3 phantom rulesThree detection rules existed as separate logic checks. All three were included as explicit model features: consecutive zero-sales day count, inventory-to-forecast ratio, and velocity drop score. The RF learns which rules matter per product type — not all phantoms fail all three rules simultaneously.
F2 PR-curve thresholdThe default threshold of 0.55 maximised precision (flagging fewer, more certain phantoms). But precision is the wrong objective here. F2 threshold from the Precision-Recall curve was optimised for recall × 2. This catches more real phantoms at the cost of more aisle checks — the right trade.
Lag featureslag_1, lag_7, lag_14 added as features. A product with zero sales today may have sold well last week — it's not a phantom, just slow. A product with zero sales across all three lag windows, with inventory showing available, is almost certainly a ghost. Temporal context makes the difference.
Supply chain integration

Why the old formula
was wrong — and what replaced it.

Probabilistic forecasts are only valuable if they drive decisions. LightGBM's μ and σ outputs feed directly into a stochastic replenishment policy that replaced the naive old system. The change is not cosmetic — it fundamentally alters how inventory buffers are calculated.

The old policy — what was wrong

The legacy reorder policy was:

Old policy (static)
Target = μ × Lead Time

This assumes two things that are both wrong in retail: (1) demand is perfectly predictable — that σD = 0, that tomorrow will look exactly like the 30-day average. And (2) suppliers are perfectly reliable — that σLT = 0, that a supplier who promised 7 days will always deliver in exactly 7 days. The formula buffers for neither. When a supplier delivers 3 days late into a Black Friday week where demand is running 40% above forecast — which is exactly when it will happen — the policy has no protection. Stockout is mathematically guaranteed.

The result: 0 safety stock units in the old policy. P90 coverage of 32.7%. $85.6M in leakage.

The new formula — what it accounts for

New policy (stochastic)
SS = Z × √(LT × σD² + D² × σLT²) × SF
Target = μ × LT + SS
Z — service level factor, tier-based (1.645 for Medium/Low, 1.960 for Premium/High)
LT — average supplier lead time in days (from supplier scorecard)
σD — daily demand standard deviation — from LightGBM's distribution output
D — average daily demand μ — from LightGBM P50 forecast
σLT — lead time standard deviation — from supplier delivery history
SF — seasonal factor — from model's demand_lift_factor feature

The formula has two variance terms inside the square root. LT × σD² captures demand uncertainty during the lead time period. D² × σLT² captures lead time uncertainty — what happens when a supplier is late. Both terms matter. A highly reliable supplier serving volatile demand needs more safety stock than an unreliable supplier in a predictable category. The old formula captured neither. The new formula captures both, scaled by the tier's service level Z-score.

Black Friday backtest result: With dynamic safety stock applied and the Champion model promoted, the policy backtest over Black Friday and Christmas showed an 8.33% reduction in stockout events and a 4.41% reduction in lost units compared to the old policy running over the same period. The formula isn't theoretical — it was validated against the hardest weeks in the retail calendar.

Why LLaMA 3.2 3B — and what it's actually for

LLaMA 3.2 3B is a 3-billion parameter language model served locally via Ollama with sub-2 second response time and zero API dependencies. It wasn't chosen for its ability to remember retail data — it was chosen for its ability to explain patterns that Python computes.

Python is better at: exact arithmetic, sorting, filtering, reading files, computing means and percentages. LLaMA is better at: explaining why Health & Wellness is harder to forecast than Meat & Seafood in conversational language, connecting "Bias -1.82" to "Post-Christmas demand collapse," adding retail domain context that would require 50 lines of hardcoded explanations if done in Python.

The hybrid architecture captures both strengths. Python computes "Health & Wellness: WAPE 23.4%, 90,343 training rows, Bias -0.10." LLaMA receives those exact numbers and explains why seasonal health trends and promotional volatility make this category structurally harder to model.

What Python doesReads CSVs, computes exact values, sorts, filters, identifies spike weeks, builds structured text summary. Never wrong on numbers — it reads them from the file.
What LLaMA doesReceives the structured text summary and explains the retail context. Adds domain reasoning: "why Black Friday breaks forecasting models" or "why ordering at P90 protects against demand uncertainty." Temperature 0.1 for facts, 0.2 for explanations.
What LLaMA cannot doSee the raw 3.27M row prediction file. Compute anything. Invent numbers not in its prompt. The system prompt explicitly says: "Answer ONLY using the data above. Do not calculate or estimate."
Why this works at scaleYou can't pass 400MB of CSV to a 3B model. Python compresses the relevant answer into a 200-word summary. LLaMA explains those 200 words. The 3B parameter constraint becomes irrelevant because the model is never asked to retrieve — only to explain.

4 engineering challenges in the LLM layer

Challenge 01 — Routing hallucination
LLaMA answered forecast questions with replenishment tool output
The model answered "Which category forecasts worst?" by inventing WAPE numbers using order quantities, with store IDs like S9999 that don't exist in any CSV
Without strict routing, LLaMA reached for the nearest available tool — replenishment data — and built plausible-sounding answers from it. Fix: A 600-line answer() routing function with keyword + regex intent detection. The WAPE handler sits at line 241, before any replenishment handler can intercept. Forecast questions hit the forecasting path first, always. Routing is explicit, not emergent from the model's understanding.
Challenge 02 — Context injection at 3.27M rows
Raw retail data cannot fit in a 3B model's context window
Asking about store-level WAPE got generic responses — the model had no actual data to reason about and invented metrics that sounded plausible but were completely fabricated
The solution is never passing raw data to the LLM. Fix: Python reads every CSV, computes the complete answer, formats it as structured facts ("Health & Wellness: WAPE 23.4%, 90,343 rows, Bias -0.10"), and passes only that 200-word summary to LLaMA. The model receives a compressed structured brief, not 400MB of tabular data. It explains, not retrieves.
Challenge 03 — Intent ambiguity across question types
Ambiguous questions hit the wrong handler, producing confident wrong answers
"What should I order?" sometimes returned demand forecast data instead of replenishment quantities — partially correct data, answering a completely different question than asked
Retail questions share vocabulary across domains — "order" appears in both forecast and replenishment contexts. Fix: Priority-ordered routing. The WAPE spike handler checks its keywords first. If it matches, the question never reaches any other handler. Each domain has a fixed keyword set and explicit precedence order — the most specific handlers are checked before general fallbacks.
Challenge 04 — Temperature calibration
High temperature produced inventive, confident, completely wrong answers
At temperature 0.7, LLaMA invented store IDs (S9999), revenue figures ($184,200 — never in any CSV), and a supplier fill rate of 87.3% for a supplier that doesn't exist in the scorecard
LLaMA 3.2 3B at high temperature treats domain jargon as creative latitude. Fix: Temperature 0.1 for all answers that cite specific numbers — WAPE values, stockout counts, supplier names, revenue figures. Temperature 0.2 for explanatory text where phrase variation is acceptable and harmless. The model adds retail expertise around Python-computed facts. It never computes the facts itself.

MLOps governance

Component-specific gates —
not just LightGBM.

Production-grade AI systems gate every component, not just the headline model. Each part of the pipeline has its own evaluation criteria because each part fails in different ways: the forecasting model drifts, the phantom classifier silently drops recall, and the LLM starts routing to the wrong handler.

LightGBM (Forecast)
Champion/Challenger CI/CD
WAPE must improve vs V1. P90 must stay 88–92%. Zero regression in Beverages or Snacks (highest impact score categories).
Random Forest (Phantom)
Recall + F2 threshold
Recall is the primary gate — missing real phantoms (FN) costs money. F2 threshold from PR-curve. Precision is secondary.
LLM (LLaMA 3.2)
Routing accuracy tests
Unit tests verify WAPE questions hit forecast handler, replenishment questions hit replenishment handler. No hallucination on known store IDs or revenue figures.
Data Pipeline
Schema + freshness checks
Column presence validation on all CSVs. Freshness check on network_master_alerts.csv. Volume sanity check — if row count drops >10%, pipeline alert fires.
2 weeks
Scheduled retraining cadence. Challenger model trained on latest data, evaluated against champion before any promotion.
12 weeks
Rolling validation window for continuous training governance. Captures seasonal variation while remaining sensitive to recent drift.
WAPE worsens > 1.5 pts → emergency retrain
P90 exits 88–92% band → emergency retrain
Volume bias > 0.5u → emergency retrain
3 gates
Champion/Challenger promotion requires all three to pass: WAPE improved, P90 in band, zero category regression. All must pass simultaneously.
